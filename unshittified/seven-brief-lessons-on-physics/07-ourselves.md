# Chapter 7: Ourselves

If the world is a swarm of ephemeral quanta of space and matter, then what are *we*? Where do our sense of self, our values, our consciousness come from?

We're part of the world we're observing. Made of the same atoms, the same light signals exchanged between pine trees and galaxies. As our knowledge has grown, we've learned we're not at the center of anything. Earth isn't the center of the cosmos. Our sun is ordinary. Our galaxy is one of hundreds of billions. Even our species sits on one small branch of life's tree.

We're made of the same stuff as everything else: combinations of the elementary particles from Chapter 4, organized by the laws from Chapters 1–6. There's no magic ingredient that makes us different from the rest of nature.

**But this doesn't diminish us. It grounds us.**

Our brains contain a hundred billion neurons, as many as stars in a galaxy, with an astronomical number of connections between them. Consciousness, free will, the sense of "I", these emerge from that complexity. We don't fully understand how, but we're beginning to glimpse it. The "I" that decides is a real thing: it's the process of an immensely complex system reflecting on itself, modeling itself, and acting. When you feel like *you* made a decision, you're right. Who else?

The scientific picture of the world isn't at odds with our inner lives. We describe reality in different languages (physics, psychology, ethics, emotion), each appropriate to its level. These languages don't compete; they complement each other. Understanding brain biochemistry enriches psychology. The passions that animate our lives nourish theoretical physics.

Our emotions, values, and loves are not *less* real for being products of evolution, shared with animals, grounded in biology. They're *more* real because of it. They're what nature actually produced: tears and laughter, loyalty and betrayal, the music that moves us, the knowledge we've built together. All of it is part of nature. We are nature, in one of its expressions.

We're the last surviving species of the genus *Homo*, a dozen curious species that evolved in Africa, closely related to bonobos. We left Africa 100,000 years ago, driven by curiosity, and made it all the way to Patagonia and the moon. Curiosity isn't against our nature. It *is* our nature.

We probably won't last long. We're not built like turtles, who've persisted for hundreds of millions of years. All our cousin species are already extinct. We're doing serious damage to the climate, and we're largely ignoring it. Civilizations have collapsed before (the Maya, the Cretans) and ours may too.

But that's okay. Life is precious *because* it's ephemeral. We're born and die as stars are born and die. We're not homeless beings longing for something beyond nature. We *are* home. This strange world (where space is granular, time doesn't exist, and things are nowhere) is the place we live. We're made of stardust, and when we suffer or feel joy, we are being exactly what we are: a part of our world.

There are frontiers where our desire for knowledge still burns, in the fabric of space, the origins of the cosmos, the nature of time, the workings of thought. Here, on the edge of what we know, in contact with the ocean of the unknown, shines the mystery and the beauty of the world.
